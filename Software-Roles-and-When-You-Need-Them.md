Software Roles and When You Need Them

# Classifying People

Paul Graham classifies people as [makers or managers](http://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html).

Jacques Mattheij breaks a team into [the starter, the architect, the debugger, and the finisher](http://jacquesmattheij.com/The+Starter+the+Architect+the+Debugger+and+the+Finisher).

Lou Adler suggests that there are [only four jobs](https://www.linkedin.com/today/post/article/20130502173937-15454-there-are-only-four-jobs-in-the-whole-world-are-you-in-the-right-one): Thinkers, Builders, Improvers, and Producers. That is, someone has an idea, someone builds the idea, someone improves what was built, and someone sustains and maintains. Everyone has a bit of each with one primary strength.

Google breaks jobs into [building, selling, and doing](http://www.google.com/about/careers/teams/). Builders are split into Engineering and Design, Operations and Support, Product Management, Developer Relations and Technical Solutions. The [Engineering roles](http://www.google.com/about/careers/teams/engineering/) include software, hardware, UX and design, network, and infrastructure. (I think that could be rewritten as software (backend), UX (frontend), and infrastructure (hardware, network, deployment and tools).)

Looking at [jobs related to software](http://www.indeed.com/jobs?q=software&from=opensearch) we see a wide range of titles including
(Entry-level|Junior|Mid-level|Senior|Lead|Principal) (Software Developer|Software Engineer|Programmer|Coder), Architect, (QA|Test) (Engineer|Analyst), VP of Engineering, CTO, Manager, Project Manager, Sales Engineer, Evangelist, Support|Operations. There are also various competencies like Java, Python, Web, Mobile, Frontend, and Backend.

My own list includes 
architect, tester, devops, tech writer, designer, programmer, support, evangelist, manager, project manager, product manager, leader, the glue, big picture guy, details guy, coach/mentor, the generalist, the specialist

# Problems with the current system
Confusion and lack of focus.

We are hiring for titles instead of roles. How does a programmer differ from a principal engineer? Technical ability, experience, leadership, and responsibility.

Between these listings and the thousands I've seen in the past decade I believe companies are primarily screening for two things: competencies and experience levels.

Competencies is a very broad category and may include a specific language, framework, or tool, as well as social and leadership capabilities.

The industry's idea of experience is weak, with "years spent doing this" as the primary indicator. There are people with minutes of experience who will outperform people with years of experience. There's also an often unspoken relation between experience and responsibility. A senior engineer has many years experience and is often expected to lead, mentor, and fix whatever is broken. Are we really seeking expertise? A certain number of implementations and experiences? Some level of responsibility?

So, you have an amazing programmer (in terms of output)--is that person your team lead, your architect, your principal? Technical skill does not translate to leadership skills. 

Software, as a profession, suffers from a lack of differentiation among the various leadership roles. There are precious few advanced technical positions, so many people will simply start at junior developer and end at senior developer. Developers who are particularly social or show leadership potential are encouraged to become managers.


Companies need to focus more on teams, which is much harder than hiring warm bodies (and even hiring warm bodies is hard). What if people leave? How do you rotate and cross-train people? [10x teams](http://avichal.wordpress.com/2011/12/16/focus-on-building-10x-teams-not-on-hiring-10x-developers/)

# So what do we need?

## Internal
Behind the curtain we have an entire organization. We need someone who can communicate outside engineering, and someone who can communicate inside engineering. You need a vision of where the product is going and when; this may be supplied by the CEO, product manager, head of sales, or any of your engineers.

## External
To help customers you need a customer advocate, probably someone on your UX team. You also need someone to help support customers in sales, setup, and general use. At a startup, everyone does a bit of everything. That's a bit vague. When do you need the customer-facing roles? When you have customers. If you're pulling a developer away from normal responsibilities 20+ hours a week, hire help.

Beyond this, we have company and team values like diversity, values (features, polish, test), social skills (general interests, empathy, optimism) and personality type ([Myers-Briggs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myers-Briggs_Type_Indicator), [Type A/Type B](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_A_and_Type_B_personality_theory), pressure and pace.

It is essential that someone communicate with other parts of the business and with customers. Do you put your best programmer or your best talker on the phone with clients? 

So, fundamentally, what do we need? Doers and support. Doers need to know what to do, when, and why. Support supplies those answers and lets the doers focus on creation.

Programmers work better when they're not interrupted. To facilitate that we funnel communications through one person who can handle externalities while others focus on creation.

So, if your principal engineer is the architect and best developer, you need a leader. Hire a social developer who can bring continuity to the team and keep people on track while the principal hacks.

If your team consistently misses deadlines, hire someone who can match input and output. Chances are, you need to increase communication and teamwork. A strong leader with technical chops or a project manager are helpful.

# Fixing the current system

Hire with a purpose. Having two architects on one project doesn't help. Having a coach for one developer is a waste of time. The coach will eventually earn their pay, but you're better off hiring them when needed.

---

I think Google and Amazon did the industry a disservice by relentlessly focusing on technical skills. Hiring a team of rock stars doesn't make a rock star team--you need people who can work together. I know people in both companies who left because of co-workers. Beyond that, the interviews can be studied for; the companies are hiring based on knowledge rather than ability. That's not bad, but I don't think it was their goal.

I see five separate traits: social, technical, leadership, determination, teaching and learning
social is your honesty, sincerity, charisma, and ability to work with others
technical is your experience, your language proficiencies, debugging skill, etc
leadership is your propensity to lead and have people follow. maybe responsibility and assertiveness.
determination is your grit. do you stick it out or get frustrated and quit? will you pull an all-nighter to fix a customer issue?
teaching and learning is your ability and desire to take in information and effectively share it with others

Software is a team sport, so I think social is of utmost importance. If a person lacks integrity or is a jerk, don't hire them. One bad apple ruins the bunch. People with bad attitudes will not be effective leaders or teachers, and likely won't learn as fast as a humble, whole person.
Technical abilities matter a lot, but this should be tempered by the individual's ability to learn. Two of the best developers I know had garbage resumes, but they can move virtual mountains. You should also be realistic about the tasks; a lot of coding is mechanical tedium rather than complex algorithmic wizardry.
If a person is a quick learner, then experience is less important.
Determination is also extremely important. I'd take a good developer that I can rely on over a great developer who is flaky.
We all want people who can lead, but a team full of leaders (or followers) is a recipe for disaster.

If you rank each trait from 1 to 10, there are 50 total points. Look for people with totals over 25. It's okay to have someone who is really high in one trait and average in the others--build your team around the missing traits.

For greater differentiation, I like the [programmer competency matrix](http://sijinjoseph.com/programmer-competency-matrix/). There's a lot to learn, and it's okay to be ignorant of some aspects of computer science; however, when hiring it is good to know where people stand.
